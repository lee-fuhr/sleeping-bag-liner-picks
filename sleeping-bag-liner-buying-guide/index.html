How to choose a sleeping bag liner

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A sleeping bag liner is a lightweight inner layer that adds warmth, protects your bag from oils and dirt, and extends its life. Choose based on your primary need: silk for weight and comfort, cotton for budget and warmth, or synthetic for quick drying and durability.

Why you need a sleeping bag liner

A liner slips inside your sleeping bag and serves several practical roles. First, it traps a layer of warm air, boosting the bag’s temperature rating by roughly 5–15 degrees without adding much bulk. Second, it absorbs sweat and body oils that would otherwise soak into the bag’s insulation, meaning you wash the liner instead of the entire bag far less often. That alone can double the life of a good down bag. Beyond warmth and hygiene, a liner makes campsite life easier. On warm nights you can sleep in the liner alone under the stars, and in hostels or bunkhouses it becomes a clean cocoon on questionable mattresses. For thru-hikers and ultralight travelers, a liner replaces a heavy summer bag, shaving significant weight.

Cotton, silk, or synthetic: which material is right for you?

Cotton liners are the budget-friendly choice. They feel soft against the skin and breathe well, making them ideal for car camping or basecamp use. The tradeoff is weight and packed size, cotton is bulky and slow to dry, so it’s not great for backpacking where every ounce matters. Silk liners are the ultralight traveler’s favorite. They pack down smaller than a fist, weigh almost nothing, and feel luxurious against the skin. Silk breathes better than synthetics and wicks moisture, but it’s more delicate and can be slippery inside the bag. It also costs noticeably more than cotton or basic synthetic blends. Synthetic liners (often polyester or fleece) excel in damp conditions. They dry fast, handle repeated washing without losing performance, and are typically the most affordable option after cotton. Fleece liners add significant warmth but are bulkier. For wet climates or winter camping, a synthetic liner is a smart pick despite slightly poorer breathability.

What weight and pack size should you look for?

Weight and pack size matter most if you carry your gear over distance. For backpacking, aim for a liner that weighs under half a pound and compresses to about the size of a water bottle or smaller. Silk liners fit this category best, while lightweight synthetic options come close. Car campers and festival goers can happily use heavier liners without penalty. Cotton and fleece liners often weigh a pound or more and pack to the size of a loaf of bread, but the comfort and warmth they provide at a fixed camp makes that bulk acceptable. Always check the manufacturer’s stated packed size rather than trusting product photos alone.

Do you need a mummy shape or rectangular?

A mummy-shaped liner tapers toward the feet and often includes a hood or drawstring. This shape hugs the contours of a mummy bag, eliminating dead air space and maximizing warmth. It’s the best choice for cold-weather camping and for people who move less in their sleep. Rectangular liners are roomier and more comfortable for side sleepers or those who toss and turn. They work well with rectangular bags and can even be used as standalone summer blankets. The downside is they create more empty space inside a mummy bag, which slightly reduces thermal efficiency. If you sleep warm and want freedom of movement, go rectangular.

Can a liner replace a sleeping bag in summer?

Yes, for many campers a liner can serve as a standalone sleeping solution on warm nights. Silk and lightweight synthetic liners provide enough coverage when overnight lows stay above 60 degrees Fahrenheit. Pair it with a pad and you’ve got a ultralight summer sleep system. Be realistic about the temperature limits. A standard liner alone adds little to no insulation below 70 degrees unless it’s a fleece or heavy-synthetic model. If you frequently camp in the shoulder seasons, consider a liner that has a dedicated temperature rating so you know when it can stand alone versus when it must supplement a bag.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a sleeping bag liner with any sleeping bag?

Yes, liners are designed to fit inside virtually any sleeping bag. Just match the shape, mummy liners work best in mummy bags, rectangular liners in rectangular bags, but rectangular liners will still fit a mummy bag, albeit with some extra fabric to manage.

Does a sleeping bag liner actually add warmth?

It does. Most liners add roughly 5 to 15 degrees Fahrenheit to your bag’s temperature rating, depending on material and thickness. Silk adds the least warmth, fleece the most. The warmth gain is real but modest, it won’t turn a three-season bag into a winter expedition piece.

How do I wash a silk sleeping bag liner?

Hand wash silk liners in cold water using a mild detergent designed for delicates. Roll in a towel to remove excess water, then hang to dry away from direct sunlight or heat. Machine washing on a gentle cycle is possible but may shorten the liner’s lifespan.

Are sleeping bag liners waterproof?

No, standard sleeping bag liners are not waterproof. They are designed to wick moisture away from your body, not to repel outside water. If you need moisture protection from the ground or rain, look for a waterproof bivy sack or a dry bag for storage instead.

What size linen should I get for my sleeping bag?

Most liners come in universal sizes that fit standard sleeping bags up to about six and a half feet long. If you are tall (over 6’2″) or use an extra-wide bag, look for a “long” or “large” version. You want the liner to slide in easily without creating fabric bunching that steals warmth.

Is a sleeping bag liner worth the money?

For anyone who camps regularly, a liner pays for itself by extending the life of your sleeping bag and reducing how often you need to wash it. Even a budget cotton liner improves hygiene and adds a few degrees of warmth, making it one of the lowest-cost upgrades you can make to your sleep system.

In shortThe right sleeping bag liner depends on how you camp: backpackers should prioritize lightweight silk or synthetics, car campers can enjoy the comfort of cotton or fleece, and anyone sleeping in shared spaces should treat a liner as essential hygiene gear rather than an accessory. Pick one that matches your typical climate and movement style, and you’ll get more years out of your sleeping bag while sleeping warmer and cleaner every trip.
